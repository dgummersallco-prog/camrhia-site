A wedding photography contract exists to protect both parties — but a vague or copy-pasted one often fails to cover the situations that actually come up in real weddings. Here's what a genuinely useful one should include.

## The basics

- Full legal names of both parties
- Wedding date, start time, and venue(s)
- Total package price, retainer amount, and balance due date
- Hours of coverage included, and the rate for any additional hours

## Coverage specifics

- What's included: number of photographers, engagement session (if applicable), second shooter status
- What's explicitly *not* included, to avoid assumption-based disputes later
- Delivery timeline for the final gallery
- Whether raw/unedited files are included

## The situations most contracts forget

- **Photographer illness or emergency**: what's the backup plan, and does the couple have a right to know who the backup photographer is in advance?
- **Weather or venue changes**: does coverage time shift if the ceremony is delayed?
- **Cancellation policy**: what happens if the couple cancels, at what point, and is any portion refundable?
- **Rescheduling**: if the wedding date changes, does the photographer's rate or availability change with it?
- **Model release / usage rights**: can the photographer use the images in their own portfolio and marketing?

## Payment terms, spelled out clearly

Vague payment language is one of the most common sources of later disputes — not because of bad intent on either side, but because "balance due before the wedding" without a specific date leaves room for two different honest interpretations. Spell out exact dates and exact amounts, not relative terms.

## A note on liability and insurance

Many venues now require photographers to carry liability insurance and provide a certificate of insurance in advance. If this applies to your venue, it's worth confirming with your photographer well before the wedding, not the week of.

## This isn't legal advice

A solid contract template is a strong starting point, but wedding photography contracts involve real legal and financial commitments on both sides. If your package price or circumstances are significant, having an actual attorney review your contract template once is a reasonable investment.
