Nearly every wedding photographer sends some version of a pre-wedding questionnaire. Very few actually get read closely enough to change how the day is shot — usually because the questions collect information that doesn't end up mattering.

## Questions that consistently matter

- Who are the key people we absolutely cannot miss capturing? (Grandparents, a sibling flying in from far away, someone with limited mobility who won't be easy to find later in the day)
- Are there any family dynamics I should be aware of — divorced parents who shouldn't be grouped in photos, estranged relationships, anyone who shouldn't be in a particular group shot?
- Is there a specific moment, tradition, or heirloom that matters more to you than a typical shot list item?
- Who's handling timeline logistics day-of — is there a planner or coordinator I should be in contact with directly?
- Are there any physical considerations I should know about — mobility issues, sensitivities to flash, anyone who prefers not to be photographed extensively?

## Questions most templates ask that rarely change anything

Generic style preference questions ("do you prefer candid or posed shots?") tend to produce vague answers that don't meaningfully change how a photographer works — most photographers already have a consistent style, and most couples chose them *because* of that style. These questions aren't harmful, just low-value relative to the space they take up.

## The real value is in the family and logistics details

The questions that actually change a wedding day's execution are almost always about people and logistics, not aesthetic preference. Who needs to be found for photos, who shouldn't be grouped together, what's the actual chain of communication on the day — these are the answers a photographer genuinely needs before showing up.

## Timing matters as much as content

A questionnaire sent too early gets vague, still-undecided answers. Sent too close to the wedding, it competes with dozens of other last-minute planning tasks. Somewhere around 4-6 weeks before the wedding tends to be a reasonable window — details are firm enough to answer accurately, but there's still time to follow up on anything unclear.
