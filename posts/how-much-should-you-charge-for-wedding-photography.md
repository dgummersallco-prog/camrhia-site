Every wedding photographer asks this question at some point, and most get an unsatisfying answer: "it depends." That's true, but it's not helpful. Here's a more useful way to actually think through it.

## Start with your real cost per wedding, not your day rate

A wedding isn't an 8-hour shoot. It's editing time, culling time, gallery delivery, client communication before and after, travel, and — often overlooked — the software and insurance that keep your business running. When photographers price based only on "hours at the wedding," they consistently undercharge.

A rough framework:

- **Shooting time**: your actual hours on the day
- **Editing time**: typically 1.5–2x your shooting hours for a full wedding
- **Admin time**: contracts, questionnaires, timeline planning, messaging — easy to underestimate, usually 3–5 hours per wedding when you add it all up
- **Fixed costs per wedding**: gear depreciation, insurance, software, a portion of your annual marketing spend

## What packages typically look like in 2026

Most photographers land somewhere in three tiers:

- **Elopement / small wedding**: shorter coverage (2–4 hours), a smaller gallery, often no album included
- **Full day coverage**: 8–10 hours, ceremony through reception, full gallery
- **Premium / full experience**: engagement session included, second shooter, album, extended coverage

Pricing within each tier varies enormously by market — a full-day package might run $2,500 in a smaller market and $6,000+ in a major metro. Rather than copying a number you saw online, price against photographers in *your* actual market with comparable experience.

## The mistake that costs photographers the most

It's not underpricing your package — it's failing to account for late payments and payment chasing. A retainer that doesn't actually get collected, or a balance payment that arrives three weeks late, has a real cost even if the number eventually shows up: it's time spent chasing, and cash flow you didn't have when you needed it.

This is a big part of why automated payment schedules — where the retainer and balance are calculated and tracked the moment a wedding is booked, with reminders that send themselves — end up paying for themselves for a lot of photographers, independent of the package price itself.

## A simple gut check

If you're not sure your pricing is right, ask: could you comfortably take on 20% fewer weddings at this price and still hit your income goal? If the honest answer is no, your price is probably too low for the actual amount of work a wedding requires.
