Wedding-day communication problems are rarely one dramatic failure. They're usually a dozen small gaps that compound quietly over months, until they surface at the worst possible time — the week of, or the day of, the wedding itself.

## What the small gaps actually look like

- A payment "sent" over Venmo that never actually got confirmed on the photographer's end
- A timeline detail mentioned once, in passing, during a phone call three months ago — and never written down anywhere either person can reference again
- A questionnaire answer the couple assumed the photographer read closely, that actually got skimmed during a busy week
- A contract revision agreed to verbally, never reflected in the actual signed document

None of these are failures of care. They're failures of *system* — information that lived in the wrong place, or nowhere at all, at the moment it mattered.

## Why this is worse in wedding photography specifically than in most service relationships

Most client relationships have room to course-correct — a delayed project, a missed email, a miscommunication can usually be fixed the next day. A wedding has exactly one day. There's no "we'll get it right next time." That compresses the cost of every small communication gap into a single, unmovable deadline, which is part of why wedding-industry stress runs as high as it does on both sides.

## What actually prevents it

Not more communication — better *location* for the communication that already needs to happen. The gaps above share a common root cause: information that lived in someone's memory, a text thread, or a verbal conversation, instead of a shared place both people could reference identically, at any time, with no ambiguity about what was actually agreed.

This is a genuinely solvable problem, but it requires treating the couple as a real participant in the system — not just someone the photographer occasionally updates. A shared timeline both people can see and edit. A payment schedule that's visible, not just tracked privately. A single source of truth that isn't anyone's memory.
