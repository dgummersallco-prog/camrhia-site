A wedding timeline built entirely by a couple or planner, without photographer input, is one of the most common causes of missed shots — not because anyone did anything wrong, but because non-photographers don't always know which moments need explicit protected time.

## Non-negotiable blocks to protect

- **Getting-ready coverage** — needs enough lead time to capture candidly, not rushed
- **Family formals** — almost always takes longer than couples estimate, especially with larger or blended families; build in more buffer than requested
- **Couple portraits** — ideally with a real window, not squeezed between two other events
- **Golden hour**, if the couple wants it — this needs to be an explicitly scheduled block, not left to chance
- **Reception key moments** — first dance, toasts, cake cutting — flag these clearly so you're never caught off guard mid-setup elsewhere

## Questions to ask before finalizing any timeline

- What time does real usable light disappear at this venue and time of year?
- Is the ceremony indoor or outdoor, and does that affect available light for nearby portraits?
- How large is the family formal list, realistically — and does the couple's time estimate match that reality?
- Is there a buffer built in anywhere, or is the schedule back-to-back with zero slack?

## Where most timelines go wrong

The most common failure point isn't a missing event — it's *zero buffer* anywhere in the schedule. A single delay (hair and makeup running late, a ceremony starting behind schedule) cascades through a timeline with no slack, and something at the end — often golden hour portraits — quietly gets cut.

## Build it collaboratively, not just reviewed after the fact

A timeline you only see once it's finalized gives you no room to flag problems in advance. Being involved in building it — not just approving a version handed to you — is the difference between catching a lighting or logistics problem in planning versus discovering it on the actual day.
