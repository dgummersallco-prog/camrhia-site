Open almost any piece of wedding photography software and you'll find the same underlying assumption: the photographer is the user, and the couple is someone the photographer manages. Contracts get *sent* to couples. Invoices get *sent* to couples. Timelines get *sent* to couples. The couple's role, in most of these tools, is to receive and respond — not to actually participate.

## That assumption doesn't match reality

A wedding isn't one person's project with a client attached. It's two people — sometimes three or four, if you count both partners and their families — actively planning something together, in real time, over the course of months. The photographer is a critical part of that planning process, especially for the day-of timeline, but they're not the only active participant.

When software treats the couple as a passive recipient rather than a genuine collaborator, the gap shows up in predictable ways: a timeline that's technically "shared" but only editable by the photographer, so any change requires a message and a wait. A payment schedule the couple can see but not meaningfully interact with. A questionnaire the couple fills out once, into a void, with no visibility into what happens with their answers.

## Why this happened

It's not a design failure so much as a reasonable starting point that never got revisited. Client-management software across creative industries — not just photography — has historically been built around the *service provider's* workflow, because the service provider is the one paying for the software. The client's experience was treated as an output of the tool, not a core user of it.

## What changes when you design for both sides equally

When a couple can genuinely *edit* a shared timeline — not just view one — and the photographer has to approve changes before they're locked in, something changes in how the planning actually feels. It stops being "the photographer's plan, shared with us" and starts being "our plan, that we built together." That's a small structural difference with a real effect on how connected both sides feel to the process.

This is the specific problem Camrhia was built to solve — not by adding more features to a photographer-facing tool, but by starting from the assumption that both people planning a wedding deserve an equal seat at the table.
