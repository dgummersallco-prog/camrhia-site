Whether to bring a second shooter isn't a decision to make once and apply to every wedding — the right call shifts based on the specifics of each one. Here's a practical way to think through it.

## When a second shooter genuinely earns their cost

- **Large weddings** (150+ guests), where more happens simultaneously than one photographer can cover
- **Weddings with separated getting-ready locations**, where the couple is preparing in two different places at once
- **Complex venues** with multiple distinct spaces (ceremony, cocktail hour, reception all in different areas), especially if timing overlaps
- **Couples who specifically want more candid guest coverage** throughout the day, which is easier to capture with a second photographer working the room independently

## When solo coverage is genuinely enough

- Smaller, more intimate weddings where one photographer can realistically cover every key moment
- Elopements and micro-weddings
- Couples on a tighter budget, where a second shooter would meaningfully increase the package price relative to what it adds

## The real cost consideration

A second shooter isn't just their day-rate — it's also additional editing and culling time on your end, since you're now reviewing two photographers' worth of images. Price this into the package accordingly, not just as a pass-through of the second shooter's fee.

## How to actually communicate this to a couple

Rather than presenting "second shooter: yes/no" as a binary upsell, it's often more useful to walk through the specific reasons it would or wouldn't matter for *their* particular day — guest count, venue layout, whether getting-ready happens in one place or two. Couples generally respond better to a specific recommendation than a generic upsell.
