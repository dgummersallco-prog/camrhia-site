Of every vendor you'll book for your wedding, photography tends to have the widest price range — and the least obvious reasons why. Here's what's actually driving the number, so you can evaluate a quote with real context instead of just comparing dollar figures.

## The rough national range

In most U.S. markets, full-day wedding photography coverage runs somewhere between **$2,000 and $6,000**, with major metro areas and highly experienced photographers going well above that. Elopements and smaller weddings with shorter coverage windows can run considerably less.

That range is wide on purpose — the number depends heavily on:

- **Hours of coverage** (a 6-hour package costs meaningfully less than 10 hours)
- **Second shooter or not**
- **Album and print inclusion** (a physical album can add $500–1,500+ on its own)
- **Your photographer's experience and demand** — more established photographers with a fuller book of business generally charge more
- **Your market** — coverage in a major city typically costs more than the same coverage in a smaller town

## What's usually included (and what isn't)

Most packages include the shoot itself and a digital gallery. What varies:

- Engagement session — sometimes included, sometimes a separate add-on
- Physical prints or an album — frequently *not* included in a base package
- A second shooter — often an upgrade, not standard
- Raw/unedited files — most photographers do not include these, and this is worth asking about directly if it matters to you

## Questions worth asking before you book

- What's your typical turnaround time for the final gallery?
- What happens if you're sick or unavailable on the wedding day — is there a backup plan?
- Is the retainer refundable under any circumstances?
- What's included versus what's an add-on?

## A note on payment structure

Most photographers require a retainer (typically 25–50% of the total) to hold your date, with the balance due before or shortly after the wedding. If a photographer's payment process feels vague or entirely manual — tracked in emails with no clear schedule — that's worth a gentle follow-up question before booking, since it's often a sign of how organized the rest of the process will be too.
