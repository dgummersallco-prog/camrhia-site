Of everything involved in wedding planning, the day-of timeline gets less attention than it deserves — and it's arguably the single document that determines whether your day feels relaxed or rushed.

## Start from your ceremony time and work outward

Rather than planning forward from when you wake up, anchor everything to your ceremony start time, then work backward (getting ready, hair and makeup, first look if you're having one) and forward (cocktail hour, reception, key moments) from there.

## Build in more buffer than feels necessary

Nearly every wedding day runs behind schedule somewhere — hair and makeup takes longer than planned, a car gets stuck in traffic, family portraits take twenty extra minutes because someone's missing. A timeline with zero buffer turns one small delay into a cascading problem for the rest of the day. Fifteen minutes of padding around getting-ready and portrait blocks is rarely wasted.

## Talk to your photographer before finalizing it

Your photographer plans their coverage — and often a second shooter's coverage — directly around your timeline. A photographer who's involved in building it, rather than just receiving it after the fact, can flag real issues in advance: not enough light for outdoor portraits at a certain hour, too little time allotted for a large family formal, a golden hour window that's about to get missed.

## The moments worth explicitly planning for

- Getting ready (per person/group, if separate)
- First look, if you're doing one
- Family formals — this one especially benefits from extra buffer time
- Wedding party portraits
- Couple portraits, ideally including golden hour if the timing allows
- Ceremony
- Cocktail hour
- Reception entrances, toasts, first dance, cake cutting
- Open dancing and send-off

## Keep it visible to everyone who needs it

A timeline that lives in one person's inbox doesn't help the people who need to reference it on the actual day — your photographer, your planner (if you have one), and often your wedding party. A shared, always-current version beats a PDF sent out weeks in advance and never updated.
