For most couples, a photography retainer is one of the first real financial commitments made toward the wedding — which makes it worth understanding clearly before you send it.

## The typical range

Most wedding photographers require a retainer of **25% to 50%** of the total package price, due at the time of booking. The remaining balance is typically due sometime between 30 days before the wedding and the day itself, depending on the photographer's specific policy.

## Why retainers exist, and why they're usually non-refundable

A retainer isn't simply a partial payment — it compensates the photographer for reserving your date exclusively. Once they've committed to your wedding, they're turning away every other potential booking for that same day. Because of this, retainers are almost always non-refundable, even if you later cancel or change photographers. This is standard across the industry, not a red flag specific to any one photographer.

## What's worth double-checking before you pay

- Is the retainer amount and due date clearly stated in the contract, not just discussed verbally?
- Is there a specific date for the remaining balance, or only a vague reference to "before the wedding"?
- What happens if your wedding date changes — does the retainer carry over, or is it forfeited?

## A reasonable retainer isn't a red flag — an unclear one might be

A 25-50% retainer with a clearly written contract is standard, healthy practice. What's worth pausing on is the opposite: a photographer asking for full payment upfront with no contract, or one whose payment terms are vague and undocumented. Clear terms, even when the retainer itself is non-refundable, are generally a good sign about how organized the rest of the working relationship will be.
