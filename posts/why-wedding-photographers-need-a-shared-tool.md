A couple books you in January. The wedding is in September.

Between those two dates, there are roughly forty interactions that need to happen cleanly: a contract that gets signed, a deposit that gets paid, a questionnaire that gets filled out, a timeline that gets built, and a dozen small updates that need to land in the right inbox at the right time.

Most tools are built to help you manage your side of that. They're good at what they do. But they don't touch the couple's side — and the couple's side is where most of the friction actually lives.

## The gap nobody talks about

When a couple books a photographer, they get an email. Maybe a PDF. If they're lucky, a link to a client portal they'll visit once and forget.

Then the wedding day arrives and someone — usually the photographer, sometimes the bride, often the mother of the bride — asks the same question:

*"What time did we say first look was again?"*

It's not a memory problem. It's a sharing problem. The information exists. It's just not somewhere both people can actually see it.

## What "shared" actually means

A shared tool isn't the same as a tool with a login for the couple.

Real sharing means:

- The couple can **edit** the timeline, not just view it
- They can see which payments have cleared without sending an email
- You can send a questionnaire and get it back without three follow-up texts
- Both sides are working from the same document — not two copies that drift apart

Camrhia was built around that idea. Not a photographer tool with a couple-facing portal bolted on — a single place where both sides work on the same thing.

## Why this matters for your business

When couples are confused, they email. When they email, you spend your afternoon writing replies instead of shooting. When that cycle repeats across eight weddings a year, it quietly adds up to weeks of overhead that never shows up on any invoice.

> A shared timeline doesn't just reduce friction. It changes what your clients remember about working with you.

The photographers who stand out aren't always the ones with the best gear or the most dramatic editing style. They're the ones whose clients feel genuinely looked after from the first email to the final gallery.

That's what a shared tool makes possible — not by automating the relationship, but by keeping it clear.
